1. LOGISTICS & DISTRIBUTION CONTRACT:

This section outlines the primary guidelines for all logistics and distribution partners involved in the supply of Surf Excel Matic 1kg to the Hyderabad Regional Distribution Center (RDC). Adherence to these guidelines is critical for maintaining active surfactant stability, ensuring fragrance confinement, and preventing cross-contamination with food stocks.

1.1. Primary Supplier Guidelines:
    a. Product Specification: All deliveries must consist solely of Surf Excel Matic 1kg as per internal product code SKU-002.
    b. Standard Lead Time: The agreed standard logistics lead time is 10 business days from the official Purchase Order (PO) confirmation to successful delivery at the Hyderabad RDC.
    c. Delivery Schedule: Deliveries must adhere to the pre-scheduled delivery windows communicated by the Hyderabad RDC inbound logistics team. Any deviation requires prior approval.
    d. Chemical Isolation & Temperature Control: Product must be transported in segregated vehicles completely isolated from edible goods or beverage containers. Transit temperatures must not exceed 30°C (86°F) to prevent high-heat chemical outgassing, powder clumping inside the plastic film, and aroma degradation.
    e. Quality Assurance: Upon receipt, product will undergo visual and olfactory inspection for powder leaking, seal breaching, and puncture damage. Any discrepancies will be documented and reported to the supplier within 24 hours.
    f. Documentation: All shipments must be accompanied by accurate packing lists, delivery challans, and relevant material safety data sheets (MSDS).

1.2. Logistics Buffers for Hyderabad Region:
    In recognition of the Hyderabad region's high environmental vulnerability, specific adjustments to logistics buffers are mandatory under defined conditions:
    a. Trigger Conditions:
        i. Temperature Peaks: When the Indian Meteorological Department (IMD) or an equivalent accredited regional weather authority issues an advisory forecasting sustained ambient temperatures above 38°C (100.4°F) for Hyderabad and its surrounding transit routes for more than 24 hours.
        ii. Extreme Weather Events: When the IMD or equivalent authority issues warnings for heavy rainfall (monsoon season), flooding, cyclones, or severe thunderstorms impacting Hyderabad or major transit corridors leading to it.
    b. Increased Buffer Actions (Supplier & Logistics Partner Responsibilities):
        i. Extended Lead Time: An additional one (1) business day buffer must be automatically applied to the standard lead time, extending it to 11 business days. This allows for potential transit delays and re-routing.
        ii. Vehicle Specification: All transport vehicles must be fully enclosed, watertight, dry-box trailers equipped with operational passive ventilation strips to prevent moisture accumulation from turning detergent powder into hard blocks.
        iii. Pre-cooling/Staging: Where feasible, product should be staged in a cool, low-humidity warehouse zone prior to loading.
        iv. Communication Frequency: Logistics partners must increase communication frequency with the Hyderabad RDC inbound team, providing hourly updates on shipment status, GPS location, and any encountered delays.
        v. Route Prioritization: Logistics partners are required to utilize primary, well-maintained arterial routes known for resilience during adverse weather, even if slightly longer.

2. REGIONAL RISK FACTOR:

The Hyderabad region, while a critical distribution hub, presents distinct environmental and demand-related challenges that specifically alter fulfillment capacity for Surf Excel Matic 1kg. Understanding these risks is crucial for proactive supply chain management.

2.1. Climate Profile and Local Disruptions:
    a. Monsoon Season (Typically June - September):
        i. Road Network Impact: Intense, prolonged rainfall can lead to severe waterlogging, flash floods, and damage to road infrastructure, particularly on secondary routes. This directly impacts transit times from supplier plants and last-mile delivery to retail outlets.
        ii. Vehicle Mobility: Reduced visibility for drivers, increased risk of breakdowns (e.g., electrical faults, tire punctures), and general traffic slowdowns significantly degrade delivery efficiency and increase transit duration.
        iii. Labor Availability: Heavy rains can disrupt daily commutes, leading to absenteeism among RDC staff, loading/unloading personnel, and delivery drivers, thereby reducing operational capacity.
        iv. Product Integrity Risk: Severe monsoon humidity directly compromises cardboard retail scoping box boundaries and can cause detergent powder caking inside individual polybags if micro-tears are present.
    b. Peak Summer Demand & Temperatures (Typically April - June):
        i. Elevated Demand: Hyderabad experiences extremely hot summers, prompting a significant surge in household washing and laundry cycles, which places immense pressure on existing stock levels and replenishment cycles.
        ii. Supply Chain Strain: Increased demand coincides with ambient temperatures frequently exceeding 40°C (104°F). Extreme heat accelerates active chemical degassing, risking pouch expansion and seal popping.
        iii. Logistics Challenges: High temperatures can cause heat-related stress to vehicle engines, increasing the likelihood of breakdowns and delivery delays.
        iv. Congestion: Peak summer also typically sees increased general economic activity, exacerbating road congestion and further impacting on-time delivery performance.

2.2. Alteration of Fulfillment Capacity:
    These regional vulnerabilities directly reduce the effective fulfillment capacity for Surf Excel Matic 1kg through:
    a. Reduced On-Time Delivery (OTD): Increased transit times and operational delays inevitably lead to lower OTD rates to the RDC and onward to retailers.
    b. Higher Stockout Risk: The combination of increased household demand and potential supply chain water disruptions significantly elevates the risk of stockouts at both the RDC and retail points.
    c. Increased Operational Costs: Contingency measures such as expedited shipping, separate non-food vehicles, or increased buffer stock inherently raise logistics and warehousing costs.
    d. Quality Compliance Risk: Prolonged exposure to non-optimal humidity can compromise product usability and chemical composition, potentially leading to returns or brand reputation damage.

3. CONTINGENCY STRATEGY:

This section defines the emergency procurement actions to be initiated if safety stock levels for Surf Excel Matic 1kg at the Hyderabad RDC drop critically due to unforeseen disruptions or demand spikes.

3.1. Critical Safety Stock Trigger:
    Emergency procurement actions will be triggered when the available safety stock of Surf Excel Matic 1kg at the Hyderabad RDC falls below 5 days of projected sales demand AND the next scheduled inbound shipment is confirmed to be delayed beyond its standard 10-day lead time. This threshold represents an immediate threat to service levels and requires rapid intervention.

3.2. Emergency Procurement Actions:
    Upon activation of the Critical Safety Stock Trigger, the following hierarchical actions will be implemented:

    a. Tier 1: Intra-Regional Stock Transfer (First Priority - within 24 hours):
        i. Authorization: Regional Supply Chain Manager (Hyderabad) with immediate notification to National Logistics Head.
        ii. Action: Identify nearest approved RDCs with surplus stock of Surf Excel Matic 1kg. Initiate immediate transfer via dedicated, expedited truck.
        iii. Logistics: Prioritize carriers with proven emergency response capabilities. Secure appropriate ventilated or protected vehicles to minimize transit time and maintain product quality.
        iv. Cost Protocol: Expedited freight costs for Tier 1 actions are to be logged against the designated emergency budget code: EMER-BUD-SKU-002.

    b. Tier 2: Expedited Procurement from Primary Supplier (If Tier 1 is insufficient or infeasible - within 48 hours):
        i. Authorization: National Logistics Head and Procurement Director.
        ii. Action: Engage primary supplier for an emergency production run (if feasible) or expedited dispatch from their finished goods inventory. Request for a 1-2 business day lead time.
        iii. Logistics: Mandate dedicated, non-stop ground transport (team drivers) as necessary.
        iv. Cost Protocol: Expedited production/freight premiums associated with Tier 2 actions require explicit approval from the Procurement Director and are subject to detailed cost-benefit analysis post-event.

    c. Tier 3: Third-Party/Alternative Supplier Sourcing (Last Resort - within 72 hours):
        i. Authorization: Executive Leadership (VP Supply Chain, VP Procurement).
        ii. Action: Engage pre-vetted and approved third-party suppliers or alternative manufacturing sites capable of producing Surf Excel Matic 1kg to our specifications.
        iii. Due Diligence: Sourcing alternatives must match performance metrics and ensure packaging safety checks are expedited.
        iv. Cost Protocol: Sourcing from Tier 3 suppliers often incurs significantly higher costs and requires executive-level approval due to potential impact on long-term supplier relationships and higher unit costs.

3.3. Communication and Reporting:
    a. Immediate Alert: The Hyderabad RDC Manager must immediately inform the Regional Supply Chain Manager, National Logistics Head, and relevant Sales & Marketing teams upon trigger activation.
    b. Daily Updates: A daily incident report detailing stock levels, incoming shipment ETAs, and actions taken must be circulated to the executive supply chain team until safety stock levels are restored to normal operating parameters (7+ days of supply).
    c. Post-Event Analysis: A comprehensive Root Cause Analysis (RCA) must be conducted within 7 business days of stock normalization to identify the underlying reasons for the critical drop and recommend preventative measures or SOP updates.
